Investment Metrics
Explained

Every number PropCompare calculates — what it means, how it's computed, and what makes a deal good or bad.

Return Metrics

Cap Rate

Unlevered return assuming an all-cash purchase. Formula: NOI ÷ Purchase Price. Ignores financing so it's useful for comparing deals apples-to-apples regardless of how you finance them.

≥7% Excellent  ·  5–7% Good  ·  <5% Review carefully

Cash-on-Cash Return

Annual cash flow relative to the actual dollars you deployed: Annual Cash Flow ÷ (Down + Closing + Rehab). This accounts for leverage and measures how hard your cash is working.

≥8% Excellent  ·  5–8% Good  ·  <5% Thin margins

IRR — Internal Rate of Return

The single most comprehensive return metric. IRR accounts for all cash flows over your hold period — annual income, principal paydown, and the net proceeds from sale — then calculates the annualized return rate at which the net present value equals zero. A higher IRR means a more efficient use of capital. PropCompare uses Newton-Raphson iteration to solve for IRR.

≥15% Excellent  ·  10–15% Good  ·  <10% Below typical hurdle rate

NOI — Net Operating Income

Effective gross income minus all operating expenses — before debt service. Formula: Eff. Income − Total OpEx. This is the property's earning power independent of how it's financed.

NPV — Net Present Value @ 10%

The present value of all future cash flows (including sale proceeds) discounted at a 10% hurdle rate, minus your initial investment. A positive NPV means the deal beats a 10% alternative investment. A negative NPV means it doesn't clear that bar.

GRM — Gross Rent Multiplier

A quick first-pass filter: Purchase Price ÷ Annual Gross Rent. Lower is better — it tells you how many years of gross rent to pay off the purchase. Doesn't account for expenses so never use it in isolation.

≤10x Strong  ·  10–15x Moderate  ·  >15x Expensive
Risk & Break-Even

DSCR — Debt Service Coverage Ratio

How comfortably NOI covers your mortgage payments: NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service. Lenders typically require ≥1.25. A DSCR below 1.0 means you're subsidizing the property from your own pocket every month.

≥1.3x Comfortable  ·  1.0–1.3x Tight  ·  <1.0x Negative cash flow

Break-Even Ratio

The percentage of gross income consumed by expenses + debt service: (OpEx + Debt Service) ÷ Effective Gross Income. Tells you how little margin you have — a lower number gives you more cushion against rent drops or unexpected costs.

≤75% Healthy buffer  ·  75–90% Tight  ·  >90% Very risky

Break-Even Vacancy Rate

The vacancy percentage at which the property stops producing positive cash flow. If the market vacancy in your area is 8% and your break-even vacancy is 12%, you have a comfortable 4% buffer. Calculated by solving for the vacancy rate that drives annual cash flow to zero.

≥20% Very safe  ·  10–20% Moderate  ·  <10% Fragile

Expense Ratio

Operating expenses as a percentage of effective gross income: Total OpEx ÷ Effective Income. Multi-family properties typically run 35–50%; single-family 30–45%. A high expense ratio eats into your NOI and cash flow.

≤40% Lean  ·  40–55% Normal  ·  >55% Bloated
Hold Period Projection

PropCompare builds a year-by-year model that accounts for rent growth and appreciation:

  • Cumulative Cash Flow — sum of annual after-debt cash flows with rent growing each year
  • Equity Paydown — principal reduction on your loan via amortization
  • Appreciation — property value increase at your assumed annual rate
  • Total Return — all three combined (does not include sale costs)
  • Net Sale Proceeds — estimated sale price minus remaining loan balance and 6% selling costs

All projections are estimates based on your inputs. Actual results will vary. Not financial advice.
